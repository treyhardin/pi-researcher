# Electrogravitic Propulsion History

## Summary
The history of electrogravitic propulsion is characterized by a transition from the early 20th-century hypothesis of gravity manipulation via electric fields to the modern, scientifically validated understanding of electrohydrodynamic (EHD) propulsion. Centered around the work of Thomas Townsend Brown and the discovery of the Biefeld-Brown effect, this field explores the use of high-voltage electric fields to generate thrust through the movement of charged particles (ion wind).

## Detailed Findings

### The Foundations: The Biefeld-Brown Effect (1920s–1950s)
The lineage of this technology begins with the experiments of **Paul Biefeld** and **Thomas Townsend Brown**. In the 1920s, they identified that an asymmetric capacitor, when subjected to high voltage, would experience a net propulsive force. This phenomenon, known as the **Biefeld-Brown effect**, was the catalyst for what Brown termed "**electrogravitics**."

Brown originally hypothesized that these electric fields could directly interact with the gravitational field, potentially allowing for "anti-gravity" propulsion. His work during this period laid the groundwork for much of the unconventional propulsion research that emerged in the mid-20th century.

### Key Historical Documents & Patents
Research into the development of this technology has identified several critical historical records:
- **British Patent # 3_.311 (1924) - "Gravitator"**: One of the earliest demonstrations of the interaction between electricity and gravitation using suspended lead balls.
- **US Patent 2,949,550 (1960) - "Electrokinetic Apparatus"**: Filed in 1958 by Brown, this patent describes devices leveraging electrical fields to produce thrust.
- **US Patent 3,187,206 (1965) - "Electrokinetic Apparatus"**: Further advancement in electrokinetic technology, assigned to Electrokinetics, Inc.
- **Office of Naval Research (ONR) Investigation (1952)**: The **Special Inquiry File #24-185** represents a significant official attempt to investigate Brown's "Electro-Gravity Device."

### The Scientific Shift: From Electrogravitics to EHD
As scientific scrutiny increased, the "electrogravitic" hypothesis (gravity manipulation) was largely superseded by the concept of **Electrohydrodynamics (EHD)**. A key tension in the historical literature exists between the term "**electrogravitics**" (the unverified hypothesis of gravity manipulation) and "**electrokinetics**" (the scientifically accepted movement of particles via electric fields).

The consensus among physicists is that the observed thrust is not a result of gravity manipulation, but rather the result of **"ion wind."** In this process, the high-voltage electric field ionizes the surrounding air (or other medium), and the resulting movement of these ions through the medium creates a momentum transfer that manifests as thrust. This phenomenon is also known as **electrokinetic propulsion**.

### Technical Mechanisms
The core mechanism involves:
- **Asymmetric Capacitors**: Electrodes arranged such that the electric field is non-uniform.
- **High Voltage**: The application of intense electric fields to induce ionization.
- **Ionization and Momentum Transfer**: The creation of a flow of charged particles (ion wind) that pushes against the surrounding medium.

### Modern Developments and Applications
Today, the legacy of Brown's work lives on in the development of **ion propulsion** and **ionocraft**.
- **Ionocraft/Ion Wind**: Small-scale, lightweight flight demonstrators that use EHD to hover or move through the air.
- **Advanced Aerospace**: Research continues into using EHD for high-altitude, long-endurance (HALE) unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) and even space-based propulsion systems, leveraging the principles of electrokinetics for precise maneuvering of small satellites (CubeSats).

## Key Entities/Facts
- **Key Figures**: Thomas Townsend Brown, Paul Biefeld.
- **Core Phenomena**: Biefeld-Brown Effect, Electrogravitics (historical), Electrohydrodynamics (EHD), Ion Wind.
- **Technological Lineage**: Electrogravitics $\rightarrow$ Electrokinetics $\rightarrow$ Ion Propulsion/EHD.
