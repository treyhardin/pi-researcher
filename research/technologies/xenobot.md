# Xenobot

## Summary
Xenobots are synthetic, living machines created by combining different types of cells from the clawed frog (*Xenopus laevis*). They are designed using computer-driven evolutionary algorithms and are capable of performing specific tasks, such as moving through a medium or collecting microplastics. They represent a convergence of synthetic biology and soft robotics.

## Detailed Findings
Xenobots are not "robotic" in the traditional sense of hardware and software, but are entirely biological. The process involves:
- **Cellular Origin:** Cells are harvested from the embryos of *Xenopus laevis*. Specifically, skin cells (for structure) and heart muscle cells (for propulsion) are used.
- **AI-Driven Design:** An evolutionary algorithm, running on a computer, simulates various arrangements of these cells to find the most efficient "body plan" for a given task (e.'g., moving in a straight line or completing a circuit).
- **Assembly:** Once the optimal design is found, the actual biological cells are physically assembled in a laboratory setting to match the computer-generated model.
- **Biological Functionality:** Unlike traditional robots, Xenobots can self-heal, use biological energy, and potentially even undergo "kinematic self-replication" (forming new Xenobots from parts of themselves).

The debate over their classification (robot vs. organism) stems from their hybrid nature: they are biological in composition but engineered and designed via computational processes.

## Key Entities/Facts
- **Michael Levin:** A prominent researcher and leader in the field of bioelectricity and the development of Xenobots.
- **Xenopus laevis (Clawed Frog):** The biological source of the cells used to construct Xenobots.
- **Evolutionary Algorithms:** The computational method used to "evolve" the optimal body plans for Xenobots.
- **Kinematic Self-Replication:** An observed behavior where Xenobots can gather loose cells to form new, smaller Xenobots.
