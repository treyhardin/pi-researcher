---# Ion propulsion


## Summary
Ion propulsion is a type of electric propulsion technology that uses electric or magnetic fields to accelerate ions (charged particles) to extremely high speeds to create thrust. Because of their high specific impulse, they are highly efficient for long-duration space missions, although they typically produce much lower thrust than chemical rockets.

## Detailed Findings
Ion propulsion works by ionizing a propellant (often a noble gas like xenon or krypton) and then using an electric field to accelerate the resulting ions out of the back of the engine. This process, known as electrostatic acceleration, provides the momentum needed for propulsion.

Key aspects include:
- **High Specific Impulse**: The efficiency of ion thrusters is much higher than that of chemical propulsion, meaning they can achieve much more change in velocity ($\Delta v$) for the same amount of propellant mass.
- **Low Thrust**: While highly efficient, the actual thrust generated is relatively low, making them unsuitable for launching from Earth but ideal for deep-space maneuvering and long-duration missions.
- **Propellant Types**: Xenon is a common choice due to its high atomic mass and ease of ionization, but newer developments are exploring iodine as a more cost-effective and denser alternative. Lithium-fed magnetoplasmadynamic thrusters are also being tested for potential use in nuclear electric propulsion.

## Key Entities/Facts
- **Xenon**: A widely used propellant for ion thrusters.
- **Iodine**: An emerging alternative propellant, offering higher storage density and lower cost.
- **NASA Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL)**: A leading institution in the development and testing of ion propulsion technologies, including recent tests of lithium-fed thrusters.
- **High Specific Impulse**: The defining performance advantage of ion propulsion over chemical propulsion.
