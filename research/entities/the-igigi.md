# The Igigi (Mesopotamian Entities)

## Overview
In Mesopotamian mythology, the **Igigi** were a group of younger, secondary deities who served the more senior **Anunnaki**. Their role in the mythic hierarchy is central to early Near Eastern cosmogonies and explains the origins of human labor in several creation myths.

## Mythological Role and the Rebellion
The Igigi are primarily known for their role in the *Atrahasis* epic. According to the myth:
- **Laborers of the Gods:** The Igigi were tasked with the heavy lifting of maintaining the earth, specifically the digging of irrigation canals and the maintenance of watercourses.
- **The Great Rebellion:** The burden of this labor became so great that the Igigi eventually rose in revolt against their masters, the Anunnaki. 
- **The Creation of Humanity:** To relieve the Igigi of their toil, the gods (led by Enlil) decided to create humans. The "blood of a slain god" was mixed with clay to create a new workforce, thereby reinstating the cosmic order and relieving the Igigi of their burdensome tasks.

## Connection to Anomalous Research
In the context of anomalous phenomena and "Ancient Astronaut" theories:
- **The Servants of the Anunnaki:** The distinction between the "ruling" Anunnaki and the "working" Igigi is often used in fringe theories to describe different classes of non-human intelligences (NHI) or extraterrestrial visitors active on Earth in antiquity.
- **The "Anunnaki" Connection:** The Igigi's rebellion and the subsequent creation of humans are often interpreted by proponents of the ancient astronaut hypothesis as descriptions of biological or technological interventions by advanced extraterrestrial beings.
