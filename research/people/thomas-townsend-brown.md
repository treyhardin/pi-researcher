# Thomas Townsend Brown

## Summary
Thomas Townsend Brown (1905–1985) was an American inventor and physicist best known for his research into the potential connection between strong electric fields and gravity, a field he termed "electrogravitics." His most notable discovery, the Biefeld-Brown effect, involves a propulsive force generated by asymmetric capacitors when subjected to high voltage. While Brown believed he had discovered a method for anti-gravity propulsion, modern scientific consensus attributes these observations to electrohydrodynamics (EHD)—the movement of charged particles (ionic wind) through the air. His work remains a foundational element in the history of ion propulsion and continues to be a subject of interest in UFO and unconventional propulsion research.

## Detailed Findings

### The Biefeld-Brown Effect and Electrogravitics
Brown's primary research focused on the phenomenon where high-voltage electrical applications to asymmetric electrodes produced a noticeable thrust.
- **Biefeld-Brown Effect:** This effect describes the thrust produced by an asymmetric capacitor. It is the precursor to modern ion propulsion and electrohydrodynamic (EHD) devices.
- **Electrogravitics:** Brown used this term to describe his proposed theory that electricity could be used to manipulate or counteract gravitational forces. He believed that intense electric fields could create a "gravitational" effect.

### Scientific Interpretation vs. Brown's Beliefs
A critical distinction exists between Brown's interpretation and the modern scientific understanding:
- **Brown's View:** He believed the force observed was an anti-gravity or "electrogravitic" force.
- **Modern Consensus (EHD):** Scientists have identified the phenomenon as electrohydrodynamics. The high voltage ionizes the surrounding air, and the movement of these ions (ionic wind) transfers momentum to the neutral air molecules, creating thrust. This is the same principle used in many modern ion thrusters.

### Historical Impact and Legacy
- **Military and Aerospace Interest:** His experiments drew the attention of various military and aerospace organizations in the mid-20th century, including investigations by the Office of Naval Research (ONR) via researchers like Willoughby M. Cady.
- **UFO Connection:** Because of the "anti-gravity" connotations, Brown's work became deeply intertwined with UFO culture and the study of unidentified aerial phenomena. He briefly served as the chairman of NICAP (National Investigations Committee on Aerial Phenomena).
- **Patents:** Brown held several patents (including in 1928, 1930, and 1965) related to his asymmetric capacitor and propulsion-related inventions.
