---# USS Nimitz 2004 Encounter

## Summary
The USS Nimitz 2004 encounter, often referred to as the "Tic Tac" incident, is one of the most significant and well-documented modern UAP (Unidentified Anomalous Phenomena) cases. In November 2004, the USS Nimitz Carrier Strike Group (CSG) encountered multiple unidentified aerial vehicles (UAVs/AAVs) off the coast of Southern California. The encounter involved radar detections on the USS Princeton and visual/sensor observations by F/A-18F pilots, including Commander David Fravor. The objects exhibited extraordinary flight characteristics, such as rapid altitude changes and high-speed maneuvers, which have since become central to discussions regarding advanced technology and national security.

## Detailed Findings
In November 2004, the USS Nimitz Carrier Strike Group was operating in the Pacific training area, approximately 100 nautical miles southwest of San Diego. 

### Radar Detections
The USS Princeton (CG-59), acting as the air warfare command ship, detected several anomalous radar contacts. These targets were observed at extremely high altitudes (between 60,000 and 80,000 feet) and were seen to descend rapidly—sometimes from 60,000 feet to near sea level in a matter of seconds. These "Anomalous Aerial Vehicles" (AAVs) were tracked by the SPY-1 radar systems for several days.

### Visual and Sensor Observations
Commander David Fravor, then Commanding Officer of VFA-41 (the "Black Aces"), and his wingman encountered a white, oval-shaped object, frequently described by pilots as a "Tic Tac" due to its appearance. The object demonstrated "instantaneous" acceleration and maneuvers that appeared to defy known aerodynamic principles. The pilots used the ATFLIR (Advanced Targeting Forward-Looking Infrared) sensor to track the object, capturing infrared footage that is now widely recognized as the "FLIR1" video.

### Timeline of Events
* **November 10–16, 2004:** The period of intense activity involving the USS Princeton's radar tracking of multiple AAVs.
* **November 14, 2004:** A core date for the primary visual encounter reported by Commander Fravor and his wingman.
* **April 2020:** The Department of Defense (DoD) formally released three Navy UAP videos, including the FLIR1 footage, to the public.
