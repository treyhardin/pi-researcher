# Igigi

## Summary
The Igigi are a group of mythological figures in Mesopotamian mythology, primarily associated with the heavens. They are often described as the younger or subordinate deities in relation to the Anunnaki. In some traditions, such as the *Atrahasis* epic, the Igigi were tasked with heavy labor (like digging watercourses) by the more senior Anunnaki, eventually leading to a rebellion that resulted in the creation of humans to relieve the gods of their toil.

## Detailed Findings
### Role and Nature
- **Celestial Deities:** In later Mesopotamian traditions (from the end of the second millennium BCE onwards), the Igigi are specifically identified as celestial deities, whereas the Anunnaki were often viewed as chthonic (underworld) deities.
- **Subordinate Status:** In Sumerian and Old Babylonian periods, the term often distinguished "inferior" divinities (Igigi) from "major" divinties (Anunnaki).
- **Labor and Rebellion:** The *Atrahasis* epic describes the Igigi as the sixth generation of gods who served the Anunnaki. Their rebellion against the burden of manual labor led to the creation of mankind.

### Etymology and Identification
- **Name Origin:** The exact origin of the name "Igigi" is uncertain, though it was historically written as *i-gi4-gi4* or *í-gì-gì*. Some interpretations suggest a numerical play on words (e.g., relating to the number 7 or 600).
- **Group Composition:** The group is thought to comprise a varying number of gods, possibly between seven and eight, with figures like Marduk occasionally associated with the group.

## Key Entities/Facts
- **Atrahasis Epic:** A primary source for the narrative of the Igigi's labor and rebellion.
- **Anunnaki relationship:** The distinction between Igigi (subordinate/celestial) and Anunnaki (major/chthonic) is a central theme in their mythos.
- **Creation of Humans:** The rebellion of the Igigi is a key mythological catalyst for the creation of human beings to perform the gods' labor.
